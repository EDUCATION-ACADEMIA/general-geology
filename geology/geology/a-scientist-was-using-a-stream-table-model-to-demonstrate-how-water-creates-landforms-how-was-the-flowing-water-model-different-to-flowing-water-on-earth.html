A scientist was using a stream table model to demonstrate how water creates landforms. How was the Flowing Water Model DIFFERENT to flowing water on Earth?

(A) ** The Flowing Water Model was different to flowing water on Earth because the stream table only had sand at the surface, but on Earth there are other types of rocks and grass at the surface

(B) The Flowing Water Model was different to flowing water on Earth because the stream table water was thicker than water on Earth

(C) The Flowing Water Model was different to flowing water on Earth because the stream table because sand is not on the surface of Earth

(D) The Flowing Water Model was exactly the same as the flowing water on Earth

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -The Flowing Water Model was different because the stream table had only sand at the surface, but on Earth there is soil, clay, and many types of rocks. Earth also has a variety of weather conditions, wind, and elements of the biosphere, such as trees and grasses that were not in the model.

Concept note-2: -Valleys and Floodplains As streams flow and carry sediment from the surface of the land, they form valleys. In high mountains, streams often cut V-shaped valleys that are narrow and steep walled. In lower areas, streams may form broad valleys that include floodplains.

Concept note-3: -How did the results of Gerya’s model provide evidence for what formed the novae on Venus? The model results matched the novae on Venus. This provided evidence that Gerya’s idea that a thinner crust and higher surface temperature might allow melted rock to push up toward the surface, forming novae.

Concept note-4: -Viscous (or sticky), non-explosive flows produce distinctive landforms known as lava domes. These circular mounds form as lava slowly oozes from a vent and piles up on itself over time. Lava domes tend to have steep, cliff-like fronts at their leading edge and wrinkle-like pressure ridges on their surfaces.
