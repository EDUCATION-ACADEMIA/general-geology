Intrusions of magma that bulge and form a dome shape.

(A) ** Laccolith

(B) Monolith

(C) Batholith

EXPLANATIONS BELOW

Concept note-1: -Laccoliths. Laccoliths are lens-shaped intrusions where magmas were emplaced like a sill between sedimentary layers but then bulged up into a dome. This commonly happens in dioritic intrusions.

Concept note-2: -A laccolith forms when magma (molten rock) rising through the Earthâ€™s crust begins to spread out horizontally, prying apart the host rock strata. The pressure of the magma is high enough that the overlying strata are forced upward, giving the laccolith its dome-like form.

Concept note-3: -laccolith, in geology, any of a type of igneous intrusion that has split apart two strata, resulting in a domelike structure; the floor of the structure is usually horizontal.

Concept note-4: -A laccolith is a form of pluton that has a convex upper roof, with a flat (or approximately flat) floor and could be said to resemble a dome in 3D (Figs. 16 and 17) (Corry, 1988). A laccolith can be classed as a type of tabular pluton.

Concept note-5: -The batholith is a large irregular mass of intrusive igneous rocks that forces themselves in surrounding strata, and laccolith is a mass of igneous or volcanic rock within strata. Batholith and laccoliths are part of igneous rocks and volcanic landforms.
